The Story Behind ClearLane

ClearLane began with a simple observation: my 2-hour BxM10 commute from the Bronx to Baruch College was my most productive study time. But when buses were delayed by bus lane violations, I lost more than travel time—I lost my rolling study hall. This personal experience led to a deeper investigation. Using data science skills developed through my studies, I analyzed 3.7 million MTA violation records to understand the patterns behind these disruptions. The results were clear: enforcement failures are predictable, localized, and disproportionately impact students during their most critical study hours. ClearLane represents the intersection of personal experience, academic rigor, and civic engagement—turning a daily frustration into actionable insights for better transit policy.

Our Research Approach

Rigorous data science methodology ensures our findings are accurate, reproducible, and actionable.

1

Data Collection

Comprehensive analysis of 3.7M MTA violation records, CUNY campus locations, and bus route data

Multiple data sources including NYC Open Data, MTA GTFS feeds, and CUNY institutional research

2

Spatial Analysis

Geographic clustering using DBSCAN to identify violation hotspots

Haversine distance calculations and buffer zone analysis around CUNY campuses

3

Temporal Analysis

Time-series analysis of violation patterns by hour, day, and academic calendar

Peak violation identification during 7-10 AM student commute hours

4

Predictive Modeling

Machine learning models to predict high-impact enforcement opportunities

Random Forest regression with 85% accuracy in hotspot prediction

5

Impact Assessment

ROI analysis and speed improvement projections for targeted enforcement

12% speed improvement potential with 40% violation reduction
